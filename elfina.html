What Founder's Office could look like at Elfina.

Founder's Office is an extension of the founder. You own what's most important but doesn't yet have a dedicated owner. You finish what the founder can't take to the end. You turn intuition into playbooks the team can run. You cover the gaps. A mini-entrepreneur inside the company.

Four things I'd want to own at Elfina

  1. Awareness for what's new.

    Couples therapy and Elfina Listens are both young, both have real stories worth telling. FO owns the awareness engine for the products still finding their audience.

  2. Less friction, first touch to first session.

    There are two onboarding flows today. FO owns the friction map, where people drop, why they wait, what they need to hear, so more of the right people actually start.

  3. Hold the 80–90%. Then beat it.

    Retention past session 4 is Elfina's whole thesis in one metric. FO owns keeping it there as the team grows, and pushing it higher, better matching signals, better check-ins, better therapist tooling.

  4. Your intuition, the team's playbook.

    Therapist screening, match calls, edge-case judgment, still you. FO writes them down, builds the rubric, trains the next hire. How 6 becomes 15 without the quality slipping.

How the engineer got made

I was a creative kid who somehow ended up in engineering. And I liked it, turns out once I'm doing something, I want to be really good at it.

I went to a low-tier private college. Not a pedigree story. But when placements came, I landed the highest offer my college had ever seen, ₹45 LPA. The only one of its kind.

That's the pattern that's followed me everywhere since: drop me into a room I don't belong in, and I'll figure out how to earn my place.

Microsoft + Uber

Six years of learning to ship

At Microsoft, I worked on the team behind product activation, the 16-digit keys that make every copy of Office, Windows, and Visual Studio actually turn on. Invisible infrastructure at planet scale. You only hear about it when it breaks.

At Uber, I built data pipelines for internal developers. My customers were my own coworkers. Fastest feedback loop I've ever had, and the clearest lesson in what "shipped" actually means.

Six years of building things other people depend on. That's the muscle I took with me when I left.

I know what I'm built for now. The room where things are being built. That's why I'm here.
